Chapter 2

Subatomic Particles

Protons: chemical identity of the atom (i.e. which element is it?)
Electrons: ionic character of the atom (i.e. is it charged?)
Neutrons: isotopic character of the atom (i.e. is it stable?)

Notation

Atomic number (Z): number of protons in the nucleus of an atom
Mass number (A): sum of number of protons and neutrons
Atomic mass: average of atomic masses, weighted for most common isotopes

Electrons

Electrons are focus in chemistry because they are involved in reactions (rather than protons and neutrons, which do not change in chemical reactions)
Exist as both particles and waves
Are described by the wave equation Ψ
Solutions to the wave equation give us Ψ2 - orbital shapes that represent ~90% change of finding an electron

Alkali metals end to lose 1 electron to be +1 and achieve noble gas electron configuration Halogens tend to gain 1 electron to be -1 and achieve noble gas electron configuration

Counting Valence Electrons

Valence electrons are the outermost, highest energy electrons
At most 8 valence electrons for most of the elements we use in CHEM 142

Carbon: 4
Oxygen: 6
Neon: 8 (complete octet)
Sulfur: 6
Helium: 2 (complete duet since only have an s-orbital)
Hydrogen: 1
Helium and hydrogen only have an s-orbital so they can have at most two valence electrons

If potassium and bromine are together, it is more energetically favorable for bromine to 'steal' a valence electron from potassium, resulting in potassium with a 1+ charge and bromine with a 1- charge. Opposites attract, so they form a salt when out of solution, held together by electrostatic forces.

Common Ions

Lose or gain electrons to get to 8 valence electrons (or 2 or 0)

Group IA: Li+, Na+, K+
Group IIA: Ca2+, Mg2+, Ba2+
Group VIA: O2-, S2-
Group VIIA: F-, Cl-, Br-, I-

Boron, carbon, and nitrogen have to lose or gain too many electrons to achieve octet status so they tend to share electrons instead.

Bonding

Atoms form bonds in ways that help them reach a "noble gas configuration" (8 valence electrons, or 2 for hydrogen and helium)

Ionic vs. Covalent Bonding

Ionic bonds: bonds between two charged atoms or molecules (ions), usually formed by a transfer of electrons followed by electrostatic attraction, resulting in a net neutral charge
Covalent bonds: shared electrons Both ionic bonds and covalent bonds can be present in a single molecule (e.g. KNO3: K+ and NO3- are ionically bonded, while the atoms of NO3- are covalently bonded)

Lewis Dot Structures

Combine atoms in a way to give each a noble gas configuration

  1. Count all valence electrons
  2. Use pairs (lines) to make bonds
  3. Arrange remaining dots to make octets

General Guidelines

  • C - makes 4 bonds
  • N - makes 3 bonds + a pair of dots
  • O - 2 bonds + 2 pairs of dots

Ionic Covalent Compounds

Use NO3-, use formal charge to keep track of electrons A nitrogen atom has 5 valence electrons Each oxygen atom has 6 valence electrons (total of 18 valence electrons for all oxygen atoms)

Naming Ionic Compounds

Positive ion (cation) is named first
Negative ion (anion) is named second (ends in -ide unless it is a polyatomic ion)

Ionic Compounds: Acids

Some common strong acids you should know: hydrochloric acid (HCl), hydrobromic acid (HBr), hydroiodic acid (HI), nitric acid (HNO3), sulfuric acid (H2SO4), perchloric acid (HClO4)
Strong acids are like ionic compounds; they dissociate almost completely in water (e.g. HCl → H+ + Cl-).

Molecules in Water

Ionic compounds are often easily separated in water
Covalent bonds are strong - they do not break in water

Organization of the Periodic Table

Row: increase in number of protons and electrons
Columns: elements with similar properties (mostly because they have the same number of valence electrons)
There are other trends that are less systematic

Metals tend to conduct heat and electricity well, but end to lose electrons (forming positive ions).
Non-metals tend to be non-conductive, dull, brittle, and tend to gain electrons (forming negative ions)
Semi-metals have metal and nonmetal properties.

Most elements are solids at room temperature
Exceptions: Hg and Br are liquids H2, O2, N2, F2, Cl2, and noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, Xe, Kr, Rn) are all gases.

Atomic Mass

The mass of an atom is very small, and has units atomic mass units (defined as 1/12 of a carbon-12 atom).
The mass listed on the periodic table is a weighted average of isotope masses.

The Mole

Reactions occur molecule to molecule, so we relate mass to the number of molecules/atoms/particles/etc. by the mole.
We can't use mass when comparing substances in reactions since the number of molecules in 1g of water is different than the number of molecules in 1g of sugar.
Avogadro's number: relates mass to the number of particles (6.022 × 1023 molecules/atoms/particles/etc. in 1 mole).
The mass on the periodic table gives the mass in grams of one mole of that element.

How many grams of O2 does 0.5 moles weigh?

Percent Yield

What causes low yield?

  • Human error: lost some substance, etc.
  • Impurities
  • Side reactions (unintended reactions, reactants reacting with substances in the air)

Redox Reactions: Moving Electrons

Oxidation-reduction reactions involve the transfer of electrons from one species to another (change in oxidation state)
Oxidation: loss of electrons
Reduction: gain of electrons

Oxidation Numbers

General Rules

  • Pure elements are zero (Na, O2)
  • Charge on a single atom = oxidation number (Na+ = +1, Mg2+ = +2)
  • The sum of all oxidation numbers in a molecule is the total charge of that molecule

Specific Rules

  • Hydrogen: +1 with a nonmetal (HCl, HF) but -1 with a metal or boron (LiH, BH3)
  • Oxygen: -1 in peroxides (H2O2) but -2 in other compounds (CO2, H2O)
  • Halogens: -1 usually (fluorine is always -1), but different when with O or other halogens above them
  • Everything else is figured out by arithmetic because oxidation numbers sum to the total charge of that molecule

NH4NO3
NH4+ → H: +1; N: -3
NO3- → O: -2; N: +5

Activity Series

What happens when we put metals in acid? A metal higher in the series will displace an element below it in the series If a metal is above H2 on the series, it will react with acid to form hydrogen gas

Balancing Redox Reactions

A properly balanced redox reaction means both mass and charge are balanced
We need to keep track of both atoms and electrons
Sometimes water is involved in the reaction

Balancing: Oxidation Number Method

Fe2+ + MnO4- + H+ → Fe3+ + Mn2+ + H2Ol
Fe loses 1 e- (goes from +2 to +3)
Mn gains 5 e- (goes from +7 to +2)
Need 5 Fe2+ atoms to provide 5 e- to Mn4-
5 Fe2+ + MnO4- + H+ → 5 Fe3+ + Mn2+ + H2O
Oxygen can be balanced by adding more H2O, and the H from the added waters can be balanced by adding H+
Final reaction balanced for mass and charge: 5 Fe2+ + MnO4- + 8 H+ → 5 Fe3+ + Mn2+ + 4 H2O

Balancing: Half Reaction Method

Another way to balance redox reactions is to separate them.

Fe2+ + MnO4- → Fe3+ + Mn2+
Iron is oxidized (loses e-): Fe2+ → Fe3+
Manganese is reduced: MnO4- → Mn2+
What about oxygen? Add H2O to balance O and add H+ to balance H, use e- to balance charge.
Fe2+ → Fe3+ + e-
MnO4- + 8 H+ + 5 e- → Mn2+ + 4 H2O
Multiply the reaction involving iron by five: 5 Fe2+ → 5 Fe3+ + 5 e-
Now we can add the two reactions: 5 Fe2+ + MnO4- + 8 H+ + 5 e- → 5 Fe3+ + 5 e- + Mn2+ + 4 H2O
The 5 e- can be canceled out since they appear in both reactants and products: 5 Fe2+ + MnO4- + 8 H+ → 5 Fe3+ + Mn2+ + 4 H2O

The previous example is finished if it occurs in acidic conditions.
However, if it occurs in basic conditions, at the end of balancing we add one OH- to both sides of reaction for every H+:

5 Fe2+ + MnO4- + 8 H+ + 8 OH- → 5 Fe3+ + Mn2+ + 4 H2O + 8 OH-
The H+ and OH- on the same side of the reaction can be combined into H2O:
5 Fe2+ + MnO4- + 8 H2O → 5 Fe3+ + Mn2+ + 4 H2O + 8 OH-
5 Fe2+ + MnO4- + 4 H2O → 5 Fe3+ + Mn2+ + 8 OH-

Atmosphere and Pressure

Earth's Atmosphere:

  • ~50km (31 miles) thick
  • 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, trace gases (argon, carbon dioxide, water vapor, methane, etc.)
  • The weight of these gases exerts pressure
    • Atmospheric pressure depends on location, temperature, weather
    • Amount of air (pressure decreases as altitude increases)

Vacuum: no gas particles present at all so Pgas = 0

Characteristics of Gases

  • Highly compressible
  • Thermally expandable
  • Low viscosity (easy to poor)
  • Low density (typically measured in g/L because densities are so low)
  • "Infinitely miscible" - can be mixed together in any proportion (solubility is not a thing since gases do not interact with each other)

Pressure Definitions

Pressure: force per unit area (P = F/A)

  • Has units N/m2 = Pascal (Pa)
  • In chemistry we use atmospheres (atm) or mmHg
  • Height of mercury in a barometer is directly correlated with pressure (P = ρ × g × h)
  • At sea level, calm weather, P = 1 atm = 760 mmHg = 760 torr = 14.7 psi

Measuring gas pressure with manometers

Gas Laws

Observations

  • Volume decreases as pressure increases
  • Volume increases with increase in temperature, decreases with decrease in temperature
  • Volume increases if amount of gas increases
  • Pressure increases if a sealed container (constant volume) is heated

Boyle's Law: P1V1 = P2V2 under constant T and n

Charles' Law: V1/T1 = V2/T2 under constant P and n, where T must be in Kelvin

Avogadro's Law: V1/n1 = V2/n2 under constant P and T

2 L of H2 + 1 L O2 reacting forms 2 L of H2O, and not 3 L of H2O, since it depends on the number of moles

Amonton's Law: P1/T1 = P2/T2 under constant V and n

Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT

  • Since V is proportional to 1/P, T, and n, PV/nT is constant (gas constant)

Assumptions of Ideal Gas Laws

  • Gas particles do not attract or repel each other.
  • The volume of the particles is negligible compared to the volume of the container

Conditions where these assumptions are no longer true because gas particles are close together and interact with each other or volume of particles is no longer negligible

  • Very low temperature
  • Very high pressure

Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP)

273 Kelvin and 1 atmosphere
At STP, 1 mol of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L

Gas Density

Density = mass/volume
PV = nRT gives us a way to measure gas density, that will depend highly on P and T
n = moles = mass (m)/molecular weight (MW)
PV = (m/MW)RT → P × MW = (m/V)RT → P × MV = ρRT

Dalton's Law

Non-interacting point particles, so increasing the number of particles increases the total pressure
Total pressure = sum of individual gas pressures = P1 + P2 + ...

Ptotal = P1 + P2 + P3 + ... = n1RT/V + n2RT/V + n3RT/V + ...
PtotalV = (n1 + n2 + n3 + ...)RT

Partial Pressures and Mole Fractions

Mole fraction (Χ): ratio of moles of component of a mixture to the total moles
Χi = ni/n1 + n2 + n3 + ...
Χi = ni/ntotal = PiV/RT/PtotalV/RT = Pi/Ptotal

Kinetic Molecular Theory

Gas laws are empirical (based on observations and experiments), but don't tell us why
Kinetic Molecular Theory is a molecular-level model that describes and predicts the macroscopic behavior of gases
Main ideas:

  1. Gas particles are so small their volume is negligible.
  2. Gas particles are in constant, random motion and constantly colliding with container walls and each other. These collisions are elastic
  3. Gas particles neither attract nor repel each other.
  4. Particle average kinetic energy is directly proportional to the temperature in Kelvin

Root Mean Square Velocity

Root mean square velocity of a gas sample can be calculated using urms = √3RT/M, R = 8.3145 J/mol K, M = molar mass in kg/mol
urms is the speed of a molecule that has the average kinetic energy

N2 at 25 °C, what is urms?
urms = √3RT/M = 515 m/s

Temperature and Velocity Relationship

Gas velocities are represented by Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution: as temperature increases, kinetic energy and root mean square velocity increase.

Mass and Velocity Relationship

As the mass of a gas increases, the distribution of velocities becomes narrower. Heavier gases have a narrower distribution of velocities and room mean square velocity decreases.

Diffusion

Diffusion refers to the process of particles moving from an area of high concentration to one of low concentration.
Gas particles are described as traveling in straight lines until they collide with surroundings
No uninterrupted paths, all paths have constant collisions
Rate of diffusion is proportional to the urms so lighter particles diffuse faster than heavier particles.

Effusion: diffusion into a vacuum, where lighter particles effuse faster
Repeated effusion allows separation of gases

Real Gases

In reality there is no ideal gas, but we're close when we are at low pressure and moderate to high temperature since the gas particles are far apart and do not interact and the size of the gas particles is negligible.
The gas laws are least accurate in high pressure because the volume of the gas particles becomes non-negligible

Chapter 6

Stoichiometry vs. Equilibrium

So far we have assumed complete reactions: the limiting reagent is used up, forming the maximum amount of product
Many reactions don't reach completion; they reach equilibrium
Chemical reactions are dynamic: they can move forward and backward

2NO2 ⇋ N2O4

Over time the concentrations of reactants and products stops changing because the forward and reverse reactions balance each other

The equilibrium of the reaction 2H2 + O2 ⇋ 2H2O lies far to the right because it leaves few reactants and mostly products.
The equilibrium of the reaction 2CaO ⇋ 2Ca + O2 lies far to the left because few products are formed, mostly reactants are present.

The Law of Mass Action

Difference starting concentrations give different equilibrium concentrations, but always give the same ratio of concentrations (K).
The Law of Mass Action: at a given temperature, a chemical system reaches a state in which a particular ratio of reactant and product concentrations has a constant value.
aA + bB ⇋ cC + dD
K = [C]c[D]d/[A]a[B]b
The value of K is constant at a given temperature, regardless of initial concentration.

The Equilibrium Constant

If we change temperature, the value of K changes.
We can use K and initial concentrations to predict equilibrium concentrations.

N2 + 3H2 ⇋ 2NH3; K = 6.02 × 10-2
K = [NH3]2/[N2][H2]3 = 6.02 × 10-2

If the reaction is reversed, 2NH3 ⇋ N2 + 3H2 then K' = 1/K
If we multiply the reaction by a factor n: 4NH3 ⇋ 2N2 + 6H2 then K" = Kn
We can also use K with gas pressure: KP = (PCc)(PDd)/(PAa)(PBb)
We can relate this to concentration through PV = nRT. Concentration = n/V = P/RT
KC = [C]c[D]d/[A]a[B]b = (PC/RT)c(PD/RT)d/(PA/RT)a(PB/RT)b
KC = KP(1/RT)Δn, where Δn = (c + d) - (a + b)
For simplicity, we use unitless values, so K is unitless. We reference concentration to 1 M and pressure to 1 atm.
Pure solids and pure liquids have concentration of 1 in the equilibrium expression, so they do not appear in the equilibrium expression.

Magnitude of the Equilibrium Constant

Since K relates products to reactants, if K is large, the reaction favors formation of products at equilibrium
If K is small, then the reaction favors reactants at equilibrium
If K is approximately 1, both reactants and products are almost similarly favored.

Types of Equilibrium Constants

  • Dissociation constants (Ka, Kb) for acids, bases, and salts
  • Solubility product (Ksp)
  • Stability product for complexation reactions (Chapter 8)
  • Standard potentials for redox reactions (Chapter 11)

Equilibrium Constant vs. Equilibrium Position

How do we know if we are at equilibrium?
Let's define Q, the reaction quotient: Q = [C]tc[D]td/[A]ta[B]tb
Q is the same as K, but can be used at any time, not necessarily at equilibrium. The value for K holds only when the reaction is at equilibrium.
Comparing Q to K allows predicting whether the reaction has reached equilibrium.

  • If Q < K, Q is too little so more products will be formed to reach equilibrium.
  • If Q > K, then there are too many products so more reactants must be formed to reach equilibrium.
  • If Q = K, then the reaction is at equilibrium.

Solving Equilibrium Problems with ICE Tables

Solving Equilibrium, Problems

  1. Start with a balanced reaction
  2. Write out equilibrium expression (K = ...)
  3. What does K tell us? Will there be more products or reactants?
  4. Set up ICE table
  5. Plug in to equilibrium expression
  6. Solve for x
  7. Plug back into ICE table
  8. Does your answer make sense?

Shortcuts when K is very small

What Affects Equilibrium Position?

  • Concentration
  • Pressure
  • Temperature

What happens to a system if we push it out of equilibrium? We can do it by adding or removing reactants or products (changing concentration).

Effects of Changing Concentration

CH4 (g) + NH3 (g) ⇋ HCN(g) + 3H2 (g)
Q = [HCN]t[H2]t3/[CH4]t[NH3]t
If we add NH3, [NH3] increases and Q does down, so Q < K so more products form.
If we remove NH3, [NH3] decreases so Q increases, Q > K so more reactants form.

Le Chatelier's Principle

If a change in conditions (a stress) is imposed on a system at equilibrium, the equilibrium position will shift in a direction that tends to reduce that change in conditions.
A + B ⇋ C + D + Energy
Add products or remove reactants, more reactants form. Remove products or add reactants, more products form.
If the reaction is heated, the more reactants will form. If the reaction is cooled, then more products will form.

Changing Pressure

  • Change the volume of the system
  • Add or remove a component of the substance
  • Add an inert substance that does not react with the reactants or products

If we add or remove a gaseous species, it is the same as changing the concentration, so we use LeChatelier's Principle.
If we add an inert gas, it changes the total pressure but not the partial pressure of each gas, so no stress is applied, and there is no shift in equilibrium.

Changing Temperature

K is constant until we change temperature. We think about temperature as energy and whether the energy is a reactant or product.
Exothermic reaction: O2 (g) + 2H2 (g) ⇋ 2 H2O + energy
Endothermic reaction: 2 H2O + energy ⇋ O2 (g) + 2H2 (g)
Exothermic reactions release heat because more energy is store in the bonds of the reactants than in the products.
Endothermic reactions absorb heat.

Increasing temperature should favor endothermic reactions, while decreasing temperature favors exothermic reactions
Exothermic reactions release energy as a product, so equilibrium shifts towards the reactants and K decreases
Endothermic reactions absorb energy as a reactant so equilibrium shifts toward the products and K increases

Solubility and the Solubility Product

Solubility is an equilibrium process affected by temperature and Le Chatelier's Principle

Lead chromate: PbCrO4 (s) ⇋ Pb2+(aq) + CrO42-(aq)
Ksp = [Pb2+][CrO42-]

Common Ion Effect

By LeChatelier's Principle, the presence of a common ion reduces solubility by shifting the equilibrium position toward the solid reactants

Qsp vs. Ksp: Predicting Precipitation

We can calculate Qsp and use it to predict if precipitation will occur.
Qsp = Ksp: the solution is saturated so no more solute will dissolve and no more solid will precipitate.
Qsp > Ksp: precipitation will occur until the solution reaches saturation.
Qsp < Ksp: solution is unsaturated so solid will dissolve until saturation is reached.

Chapter 7

Acids and Bases

Acids: first recognized as substances that taste sour
Bases: taste bitter, can feel slippery

Lye (sodium hydroxide) reacts with lipids and oils to make soap molecules

Arrhenius Theory

Acids: substances containing H+ and whose aqueous solution contains more H+ ions that OH- ions.
HA ⇋ H+(aq) + A-(aq)
Bases: substances containing OH- and whose aqueous solution contains more OH- ions that H+ ions.
BOH ⇋ B+(aq) + -OH(aq)

Bronsted-Lowry Theory

Expands on Arrhenius Theory by describing acid-base reactions as proton transfers.
Acids: proton donors
Bases: proton acceptors

Lewis Acids and Bases

Acids: electron-pair acceptor (usually an empty orbital)
Bases: electron-pair donor (lone pair)

Acids and Bases in Water

Acid: HA + H2O ⇋ H3O+ + A- (acid donates proton to water)
Base: B + H2O ⇋ BH+ + OH- (base grabs a proton from water)

Acid Strength

Like electrolytes, we can classify acids (and bases) as weak or strong based on how much they dissociate in water.
HA ⇋ H+ + A- (for strong acids, K is large so a lot of H+ and for weak acids, K is small so mostly HA)

Strong Acids

HA + H2O ⇋ H3O+ + A-
Forward reaction dominates
H2O is a stronger base than A- (it wins the proton)
HA is a stronger acid than H3O+ (it gives up the H+ more readily)
[H3O+]eq = [A-]eq = [HA]initial

Weak Acids

HA + H2O ⇋ H3O+ + A-
Reverse reaction dominates
A- is a stronger base than H2O
HA is a weaker acid than H3O+
[H3O+]eq = [A-]eq << [HA]initial

Acid Equilibrium

HA + H2O ⇋ H3O+ + A-
Ka = [H3O+][A-]/[HA]

Base Strength

B + H2O ⇋ BH+ + OH-
Kb = [BH+][OH-]/[B]
Strong base: [OH-], [BH+] are large, Kb is large
Weak base: [B] is large, Kb is small

Strong and Weak Bases

Soluble compounds containing O2- or OH- are strong bases. The cations are usually those of the most active metals
M2O or MOH (where M= group 1A metals)
MO or M(OH)2 (where M= group 2A metals)
Many compounds with an electron-rich nitrogen are weak bases (none are ARrhenius bases). The common structural feature is an N.

Conjugate Acids and Bases

Conjugate acid: formed when a proton is transfered to the base
Conjugate base: what is left of the acid after the proton is transferred
Conjugate acid/base pair only differ by a proton
Every acid has a conjugate base and every base has a conjugate acid

Acid/Base Strength and Conjugates

The weaker the acid, the stronger the conjugate base is. The stronger the acid, the weaker the conjugate base is.
Weak acids don't let go of the proton, so the conjugate base must be stronger than H2O
Strong acids let go of the proton easily, so the conjugate base must be weaker than H2O

Water: An Acid and a Base

Water is amphoteric because it is both an acid and a base.
H2O + H2O ⇋ H3O+ + OH-
One of the waters is an acid and the other is a base.
K = [H3O+][OH-] = Kw = 1.0 × 10-14 at 25 °C
Only a small amount of water dissociates
[H3O+] = [OH-] = 1.0 × 10-7 M

Kw

Acid (Ka): HA(aq) + H2O(l) ⇋ H3O+(aq) + A-(aq)
Base (Kb): A-(aq) + H2O(l) ⇋ HA(aq) + OH-(aq)
If we add the two reactions, we get H2O + H2O ⇋ H3O+ + OH- and Kw = Ka × Kb

Expressing Acidity: pH

pH = -log10[H3O+]
[H3O+] = 10-pH
A solution with pH 7 has the same [H3O+] as pure water, but it doesn't mean that there is no acid present.

Salts and their Effect on pH

Conjugate base of a weak acid will form a basic solution in water

Ex: NaF, KCN, NaC2H3O2, Na3PO4

Conjugate acid of a weak base will form an acidic solution in water

Ex: NH4Cl, R3N where R = carbon groups or H
Also: small, highly charged metal ions (Al3+, Fe3+)

No effect on pH: cations of strong bases (Na+, K+, Mg2+) and anions of strong acids (Cl-, Br-, I-, NO3-, SO42-, ClO4-)

Small, Highly Charge Metal Ions in Water

With highly charged metals, the electron density is drawn toward the charged metal, so nearby waters act as bases and pull a proton from the metal compound.

Polyprotic Acids

Diprotic acid H2SO4 loses a proton, producing its conjugate base, which has a much lower Ka value.

Solving Acid-Base Equilibria Problems

  1. List the major species in solution.
  2. Look for reactions that can be assumed to go to completion, such as strong acids/bases dissociating or H+ reacting with OH-.
  3. For a reaction that can be assumed to go to completion, determine the concentrations of the products and write down the major species in solution after the reaction.
  4. Look at each major component of the solution and decide whether it is an acid or a base.
  5. Pick the equilibrium that will control the pH. Use known values of the dissociation constants for the various species to determine the dominant equilibrium.
    • Write the equation for the reaction and the equilibrium expression.
    • Set up the I.C.E. table and define x.
    • Compute the equilibrium concentrations in terms of x.
    • Substitute the concentration at equilibrium into the equilibrium expression expression and solve for x.
    • Check the validity of the approximation.
